Multimodal RAG: Why, What, and How

By Andrea Melloncelli | December 31, 2025

Introduction

With RAG your AI agents can search in any knowledge base: documents, emails, data bases, but exploiting only textual part. Multimodal RAG is the further enancement that allows your system to give a meaning to images, audios, etc… and to search among them. In this article we explain why to use a multimodal RAG is important, what it is and how it wolks.

Why Multimodal RAG?

Wheather you are a product manager or a researcher, as a humans you have libraries with vast collections of books, and systems that enable efficient searching within these collections, the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pattern serves a similar purpose for natural language processing models. RAG assists in locating relevant information from various sources, much like how a library system helps find specific books or pieces of information within them.

With RAG you can search in any knowledge base: PDFs and other documents, data bases, but all that have a textual form. Multimodal RAG is the further enancement that allows your system to give a meaning to images, audios, etc… and to search among them. This is the gap that Multimodal RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) fills. It’s not just about searching more data, it’s about connecting the dots across formats, so you get the full picture, not just a summary of the text.

Why does this matter?

  • Richer answers: You get context from all available evidence, not just text.
  • Fewer blind spots: Visuals and structured data are no longer ignored.
  • Real-world workflows: Most business and research knowledge is multimodal by nature.

How Does It Work?

  1. Query Understanding: The agent analyzes your question to determine what types of data might be relevant (text, image, table, etc.).
  2. Multimodal Retrieval: Specialized retrievers fetch the most relevant chunks from each data type.
  3. Fusion & Synthesis: The agent combines the retrieved evidence—text, images, numbers—into a single, coherent answer.
  4. Citations: Each part of the answer is linked back to its source, so you can trust and verify.

Example: A Day in the Life

Suppose you ask: “What were the main findings in last quarter’s report?”

  • The agent pulls the executive summary (text),
  • extracts key numbers from a table,
  • and describes the main chart (image).

You get a single answer, with links to each source.

Conclusion

Multimodal RAG is about making AI agents more useful in the real world—where knowledge is everywhere, and not always in plain text. It’s a step toward agents that see, read, and understand as broadly as we do.
